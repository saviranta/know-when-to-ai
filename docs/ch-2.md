# Chapter 2 — What this book is not

A method book that refuses to disavow the adjacent genres will be read as those genres. Three shelves are waiting for this one: the AI strategy deck, the vendor evaluation guide, the manifesto. None of them is where the book belongs. Naming that plainly is cheaper now than correcting it later.

What follows is the disavowal, in order.

## Not an AI strategy deck

Strategy decks describe a destination. This book teaches a route.

The genre convention is familiar. A deck opens with an exponential curve, names a transformation, and ends with a maturity model positioned two rungs below any named destination. Between those pages sits a set of ambition statements — *become AI-native*, *reimagine the operating model*, *unlock the next S-curve* — which have the grammar of claims and the content of mood lighting. You already know the deck. You may have paid for it.

The destination posture is a genre tell. Destinations make it easy to sell an outcome; routes make it possible to reach one. A destination deck does not have to say which problems AI is not for, because the entire document is pre-committed to the proposition that it is for this one. A route — a method — must say.

Broussard names the underlying pattern: *technochauvinism*, the default belief that computational solutions are inherently superior to non-computational ones (Broussard, 2018) [verified]. Most strategy decks do not argue for this belief. They assume it and build the rest on top. The deck carries from ambition to technology to roadmap without pausing on whether the problem was technical to begin with.

This book argues the opposite default. It argues that the honest first answer to *should we use AI here?* is *probably not — show cause*. That inversion is the single hardest piece of discipline in the method. It is also the piece that saves the most engagements. Strategy decks cannot perform the inversion, because the deck's commercial logic requires that the answer be yes.

Two consequences follow. No maturity model appears anywhere in this book. No transformation narrative frames the six stages. Maturity models are a genre device for positioning an audience below an aspiration and selling the staircase; the method does not need that staircase, because the method works on a single engagement the first time it is used.

## Not a vendor evaluation guide

Vendor comparisons age in months. The best-in-class chart in any printed AI book is wrong by the time the book ships. The honest response is not a disclaimer on the first page; it is to refuse the genre.

No vendor is named in the main text. The atlas lists tools — methods, techniques, frameworks — not products. Where a tool is vendor-bound in practice (a proprietary retrieval-augmented generation service, say, or a particular evaluation platform with no open-source equivalent), the atlas entry says so and flags it with the vendor-origin tag from the reference policy. What the book offers is not a shortlist but a decision question that outlives every shortlist.

The guide genre has a second problem beyond shelf-life. Vendor-comparison writing tends to smuggle in the frame of *which AI tool to buy* at a point in the method where the prior question — *should a tool be bought at all* — has not been answered. That is a routing error dressed as a purchasing decision. [Chapter 8](ch-8.md) spends its budget on this exact mistake.

Benchmark tables belong to the same family. They rank models against each other on tasks that have not yet been chosen as the tasks the model will do. A benchmark score is a comparison artefact, not a framing artefact. It can settle a choice between two candidates once the frame is fixed; it cannot fix the frame. Any book that leads with benchmarks has inverted the order of operations.

The atlas compensates where it can. Each entry carries its phase, its pillar, its position on the AI Ladder, and the conditions under which it earns its keep. None of that information expires when a model family is retired. The entries will still be correct when the current market leaders have been replaced, because the entries describe the problem the tool solves, not the brand that currently solves it best.

## Not a manifesto, for or against

Two gates sit above the method. Neither is a position. Both are a discipline.

The two Tier-0 gates — Broussard's technochauvinism check and Weizenbaum's judgment-vs-calculation test — are questions, not classifiers, and [Chapter 4](ch-4.md) carries them in full (Broussard, 2018; Weizenbaum, 1976) [verified]. They do not tell you to like or dislike the machine. They tell you to answer two questions before you decide to use one.

Manifestos run in the opposite direction. The pro-AI manifesto announces a destination and treats caveats as friction. The anti-AI manifesto announces a refusal and treats any use as complicity. Both are forecast genres wearing principle costumes. Both skip the work of asking which decision, today, needs which discipline.

The two gates are compatible with a range of outcomes. For many decisions, the gates pass and the method routes the problem through the atlas. For some decisions, the gates find that the problem is not technical and route it back to process or people. For a narrow class of decisions — welfare-eligibility scoring, immigration-risk assessment, predictive policing, automated sentencing support — the most defensible recommendation is to leave the category of decision un-automated (McQuillan, 2022) [verified].

That last option is worth saying out loud. McQuillan argues, from the record of bureaucratic-violence cases, that certain decision categories should be abolished rather than governed. The argument is contested. It is also serious, and a method that cannot expose it as an available recommendation is a method that has pre-committed to a narrower conclusion than the evidence supports. The method in this book exposes it. The book does not, however, treat abolition as the default recommendation for every decision. It is the default for a narrow class, named on the evidence, and it is one of four Tier-0 outcomes the gates can produce.

A method that can produce *abolish*, *judge*, *route back to process*, or *proceed* is not a manifesto. A book that can produce only one of those answers is.

Two assumptions that often travel with AI discourse are also refused here. The first is the assumption that a language model reasoning from principles is doing what it appears to be doing. Bender and colleagues make the deflationary case precisely: large language models are statistical mimics of text (Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major & Shmitchell, 2021) [verified]. The appearance of reasoning is a useful property of the output; it is not evidence about the process. The method treats model outputs as outputs, not as arguments, and it routes decisions that require arguments elsewhere. The second assumption is that the environmental and material costs of inference are settled in an appendix rather than in the framing decision. They are not. They belong in the framing decision.

## Not a forecast

No model is named as the winner. No capability threshold is predicted. No timeline is set for any transition.

Forecasting has a bad record in this field, and a worse one the closer the forecast gets to actual dates. More importantly, forecasting is the wrong shape of knowledge for a decision that lands next Tuesday. The Tuesday decision needs a framing, a routing, and a named owner. It does not need a position on which frontier model will lead in 2029.

The method is model-agnostic for a concrete reason. The gates sit above the model. The framing does not depend on which family of model is current. The routing matrix in [Chapter 8](ch-8.md) names rungs — rules, statistics, classical machine learning, language-model feature, retrieval-augmented generation, single agent, tool-using agent, multi-agent — not vendors of rungs. When the frontier shifts, the routing matrix is unchanged. The tool at the rung may update; the rung does not.

The atlas is structured the same way. Each entry declares the rung the tool sits on. A practitioner reading the book three years from now can substitute the then-current canonical implementation at the same rung and the entry still reads correctly. That is what a method earns by refusing to forecast: it ages in years rather than months.

One caveat. *Not a forecast* is not the same as *not an opinion*. Where the evidence supports a recommendation, the book makes one. Where it does not, the book says so. That is discipline, not hedging.

## What it is

A method, with a toolbox. Read once, cover to cover, in the order the engagement runs. Returned to often, by section, when a specific stage is live.

The method is six stages: frame, diagnose, decompose, route, sequence, commit. Above the six stages sit the two gates. Below the six stages sits the atlas — a reference of tools, each with a purpose, an anatomy, a generic worked example, a set of pitfalls, and a line on when not to use it.

This book reads first as a method, then as a reference. First pass, cover to cover, to see the shape. Tenth pass, a single stage before a decision meeting. Both uses are intended. Neither requires agreement with any prediction about where the field is going.

## A note on who benefits from each genre

A strategy deck benefits the seller of strategy. The deck's destination must be large enough to justify the engagement fee, and the maturity model must place the buyer far enough from the destination to justify the staircase.

A vendor guide benefits the vendors. Comparison tables drive procurement, and procurement is where the guide pays for itself — in referrals, in benchmark-co-authorship, in the next edition's sponsorship.

A manifesto benefits the author. A strong position gets cited, invited, quoted, and sold to audiences who already agree. The author's brand is the product; the manifesto is the advertisement.

A method benefits the work. It is neutral on whose work — an organisation's, a client's, one's own engagement — but not neutral on the work itself. The work is what the method serves. If the method is good, the work gets better. If the method is bad, the work gets worse in a way the method makes visible.

That last line is the test. A method that cannot be wrong about a specific engagement is not a method; it is a style. The one in this book can be wrong, in named ways, at named gates. The chapters that follow show where.

On to the shape of the method itself.
